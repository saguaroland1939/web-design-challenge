Discussion
Figures 1-3 depict the distribution of saguaro heights measured in 2013, 2015, and 2020. For the purposes of this analysis, only the 525 saguaros that were measured in all three years are included. Due to this, you can see that the distribution of heights generally shifts from left to right (smaller to larger) across the three years as the saguaros grew between measurements. The distributions would appear slightly different if all measured saguaros were shown, however, some general observations can be made.

All three distributions appear to be bi-modal. In addition to there being quite a few 'middle-aged' saguaros in the 10 to 20-ft range, there is also a large group of very young saguaros in the 1 to 4-ft range. The Garden is a museum charged with maintaining a collection of educational plant specimens. The realities of plant collection work might partially explain this distribution. Since 1939, the Garden has built up and maintained its saguaro collections from plants and seeds obtained from field collection trips, plants obtained from growers, and plants received as donations from the community. Given the risk and great expense of tranplanting large saguaros, collection efforts are focused on seeds and smaller specimen. This might account for the large number of young saguaros at the Garden.

Another factor that might contribute to this bi-modal distribution is the fact that the saguaro population at the Garden is reproducing naturally. During the annual inventories, many new juvenile saguaros are discovered by volunteers as they grow large enough to emerge from the nurse plants and boulders that protect them. For the past eight years, these "baby" saguaros, as they are fondly called, have been discovered at a rate that more than offsets annual saguaro mortality at the Garden. Therefore the natural reproduction of saguaros at the Garden likely also contributes to the bi-modal distribution.
