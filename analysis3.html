Is saguaro mortality related to elevated night-time temperatures?

What we found
Figure 7 shows the total number of nights above (>=) 90 recorded each year. Figure 8 shows the number of saguaros recorded as lost in each year. To explore the relationship between nights above 90 and saguaro mortality, we obtained daily low temperature measurements from a weather station within five miles of the Garden. For each year, we counted the number of nights above 90 F. We compared saguaro mortality in a given year to the temperature results for one year previous (Fig. 9) as well as two years previous (Fig. 10) in case there was a lag between temperature conditions and saguaro mortality. Because cacti cannot "breathe" when the night-time temperature stays above 90, we expected to find a positive relationship between saguaro mortality and nights above 90 in at least one of the lag scenarios.

We were disappointed to find no significant correlation between saguaro mortality and nights above 90 for either lag scenario. However, although the results are not statistically significant, it is interesting to see that the directionality of the relationship switches between the one-year lag scenario (Fig. 9) and the two-year lag scenario (Fig. 10).

We are surprised by the lack of correlation given the number of saguaros lost in the Phoenix community this summer (with 23 nights above 90 F) and given the known physiological impact of nighttime lows on cactus health. We plan to review the inventory data to check that all saguaro deaths were recorded in the year they occurred. If no errors are found, perhaps the 90-degree threshhold isn't as important as we had assumed or the number of nights in a year above that threshhold is not a useful metric. We could, for example, count the number of consecutive nights and modify the temperature threshhold.
